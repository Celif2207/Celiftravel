Tanzania: Lions, Spices & the Trip That Changed Everything

By Celif Tanzania 15 min read

There are trips you plan for months, and then there are trips that simply grab you by the soul and never let go. Tanzania was the latter for us. From the moment our plane touched down in Dar es Salaam, we knew this was going to be different from anything we had ever experienced before.

We had been dreaming about Africa for years. Safari documentaries on lazy Sunday evenings, Instagram reels of endless golden plains, friends coming back with that look in their eyes — the one that says "I've seen something I can't quite put into words." We finally booked it on a Tuesday night at 11pm, slightly impulsive, completely worth it.

The safari: where words fail

We joined a three-day camping safari through the Serengeti and Ngorongoro Crater. Nothing — absolutely nothing — could have prepared us for the scale of it. On our very first morning, before we'd even finished our coffee, our guide Moses pointed silently to the left. A lion. Maybe ten metres away. Lying in the grass like he owned the entire planet (which, to be fair, he probably does).

The Serengeti moves. That's the thing nobody tells you. It's not a zoo. It's not a park. It's an ecosystem that is alive in every single direction you look. Wildebeest thundering past your jeep. Elephants crossing the road with a dignity that makes you feel very small. Giraffes stretching towards acacia trees in the golden afternoon light, moving in slow motion like the world runs at a different speed for them.

The Ngorongoro Crater was equally jaw-dropping — a collapsed volcano that now holds an entire self-contained world inside it. We spotted the Big Five in a single day. We kept saying "is this real?" and honestly, we still ask ourselves that question now, sitting at home in Germany looking at the photos.

Safari tips for adventurers
  • Book a camping safari — it's cheaper than lodges and you wake up to the actual sounds of the wild
  • Go in the dry season (June–October) for the best wildlife sightings
  • Bring a good zoom lens — animals don't care about your phone camera range
  • Trust your guide completely — Moses changed our route three times and found us a cheetah each time
  • Budget around $150–250 per person per day for a decent safari group tour

Zanzibar: paradise found

After the raw intensity of the safari, Zanzibar felt like the universe offering us a reward. White sand so fine it squeaks under your feet. Water so clear and warm you forget you're in the ocean and not a swimming pool. We dropped our bags at a small guesthouse in Stone Town and immediately walked straight into the sea with our clothes on. We had zero regrets.

Stone Town — a city that tells stories

Stone Town is a UNESCO World Heritage Site and once you walk its narrow alleyways you immediately understand why. Arabic architecture, carved wooden doors that are centuries old, the smell of cloves and cardamom drifting out of tiny spice shops. We got completely lost twice and it was the best thing that happened to us both days.

The sunsets at Forodhani Gardens are the stuff of legend. Every evening, locals set up food stalls right on the waterfront and the whole town comes alive. Grilled seafood, sugarcane juice, Zanzibar pizza (which is nothing like Italian pizza and absolutely everything you need in life). We ate there three nights in a row.

"Zanzibar pizza at sunset, feet in the sand, not a single plan for tomorrow. That's the good life."

The food: an adventure on its own

Tanzanian food deserves its own article — and we will write one. But for now: pilau rice spiced with cardamom and cloves, nyama choma (grilled meat eaten with your hands around a communal table), coconut fish curry that ruins all other fish dishes for you permanently, and the freshest tropical fruit you will ever put in your mouth.

Street food in Dar es Salaam was one of our highlights. We ate mandazi (fried dough) for breakfast every morning from a woman who set up her stall at 6am outside our guesthouse. By day three she was saving us the best ones. By day five she was family.

Food tips you actually need
  • Eat where locals eat — if it's empty, walk past; if it's full of locals, sit down immediately
  • Try urojo (Zanzibar mix soup) — it sounds odd, it tastes extraordinary
  • The seafood in Zanzibar is incredibly fresh — order whatever was caught that morning
  • Drink the fresh coconut water straight from the coconut — it's everywhere and it's perfect
  • Avoid tourist restaurants near beaches — the food quality drops and the prices triple

Practical info for adventurers

What you need to know
  • Visa: get an e-visa online before you go at immigration.go.tz — costs $50 USD
  • Best time to visit: June to October (dry season) for safari, year-round for Zanzibar
  • Getting around: domestic flights between Dar es Salaam and Zanzibar take 20 minutes (~$60)
  • Budget: Tanzania is not the cheapest African destination — plan $80–120/day outside safari
  • Safety: we felt completely safe throughout — use common sense as you would anywhere
  • Yellow fever vaccination is required — check all health requirements before booking

Would we go back?

Without a single second of hesitation. Tanzania got under our skin in a way that very few places ever have. There is something about standing in the Serengeti at dawn, watching the light turn the grass gold while a family of elephants moves silently in the distance, that recalibrates everything. Your problems feel smaller. Your gratitude feels bigger. Your list of places to return to gets one destination longer.

We left a piece of our hearts in Tanzania. We're already plotting how to get it back.

"If Africa is calling you — pick up. Trust us on this one."
